Net Zero by 2050

To keep global warming to no more than 1.5Â°C emissions need to be reduced by 45% by 2030 and reach net zero by 2050. But how close are we in reaching our net zero commitments? The NZE (Net Zero Emissions) Goal made by the United Nations in the 2015 Paris Agreement legally requires countries to work together to enable a worldwide transition away from fossil fuels to renewables for energy. But is the world on track to reach our NZE Goal by 2050?

The world map below shows the share of primary energy from each country that is derived from renewable energy. Most countries still generate less than half their energy using renewables, which is concerning as the adoption of renewables will need to significantly increase in order to reach NZE by 2050. Countries in Africa, Northern Europe and South America are leading the way with the highest share of renewables in their energhy mix.

In order to reach NZE by 2050, worldwide action is needed to reduce carbon emissions and slow global warming. Although the effects of global warming is felt across the world, some countries release significanty more emissions than others. So which countries are responsible for releasing the most amount of carbon emissions? The top emitter in the world is China, followed by United States and India. Contrary to expectations, rich countries with high GDP per capita do not necessarily release more emissions. In the left scatter plot, LEDC countries are coloured in light green dots and MEDC countries are coloured in darker green dots (based on a threshold earning of $10,000/capita GDP). Most MEDC countries consume more than 40,000 TWh of energy per person in a year. Within MEDC countries, China is the highest emitter releasing more than 10 million metric tonnes CO2/person per year.

A Snapshot of Energy in Australia

Australia has one of the top ranking carbon emissions per capita, and traditionally has relied on fossil fuels for its energy. In recent years, there has been an increased uptake of renewables in the country, with around 11% of energy sourced from solar, wind or other forms of renewable energy in Australia.

In Australia, fossil fuels still make up the majority of electricity generation compared to renewables. However, electricity generation from fossil fuels has slowly decreased with planned closures of almost all coal mines in the country.
